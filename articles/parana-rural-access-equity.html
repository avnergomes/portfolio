What this is

A spatial-equity study of rural Paraná. Roughly 1.26 million people, about 11 percent of the state, live in the countryside and depend on three parallel public networks: primary care from the Sistema Único de Saúde (CNES), rural schools from INEP, and agricultural extension from IDR-Paraná. Coverage is universal on paper. In practice, a family in one municipality can reach a health post in minutes while a family in another faces a long drive over unpaved roads. This project turns "everyone is covered" into a measured question: relative to its rural population, how well served is each municipality, and where do the gaps stack up across all three services at once.

The answer is a composite accessibility score for every municipality, ranked into quintiles, published as a static choropleth and an interactive map, and documented with a bilingual write-up and a one-command pipeline that reproduces the whole thing from raw public data.

The full analysis is published as an interactive report, in Portuguese and English, with a switchable map of the composite and each service, the scatter that reveals how the three networks co-vary, a robustness panel, and a sortable ranking of every municipality. Inside the analysis

  • Three service networks. 3,075 primary-care facilities from CNES, 1,187 active rural schools from the INEP Censo Escolar 2024, and 414 IDR-Paraná extension offices, pulled from the institute's official unit shapefile and filtered to the extension network only.
  • Demand where the people are. Rural population per municipality from the 2022 Census (IBGE SIDRA table 9923), so a service is scored against the countryside it is meant to serve, not the whole population.
  • A composite, not a single metric. Each service is scored on its own, converted to a percentile rank, and averaged into an equal-weight composite. Municipalities are then ranked into quintiles, and the bottom fifth is flagged as underserved.
  • The finding under the map. Primary-care access and extension access co-vary across the state (they concentrate in the same more-developed north), while rural-school access is the mirror image, strongest in the center-south. The worst-off fifth is where health and extension are both thin at once, and those places are led by Tibagi in the Campos Gerais interior and a cluster along the Itaipu reservoir and the Paraguay and Argentina border.
  • Two views, one dataset. A portfolio-grade static choropleth for slides, and an interactive Folium map for exploration, both generated from the same scored GeoPackage.

Methodology

Access is measured with an Enhanced 2-Step Floating Catchment Area (E2SFCA, Luo and Qi, 2009), the current standard in the peer-reviewed Brazilian health-geography literature. It captures two effects that a naive facilities-per-capita ratio misses: cross-boundary catchment, since a health post just outside a municipal line still serves that municipality, and supply competition, since a facility is shared by everyone who can reach it. Distance is great-circle from each municipal centroid, with Gaussian decay (beta = 30 km) and a 50 km catchment cutoff. The three per-service scores are combined with equal weights, a normative choice grounded in the constitutional framing of health, education and extension, and the sensitivity to that choice is reported honestly rather than assumed away.

Every dataset is official and public. The CNES facilities come live from the Ministério da Saúde open-data API; INEP schools are anchored to the municipal centroid because the 2024 microdata no longer ships coordinates; the IDR-Paraná layer is the institute's own georeferenced unit shapefile. The full pipeline caches each source to disk and rebuilds the maps from scratch with a single command, and the code and methodology are open source.
