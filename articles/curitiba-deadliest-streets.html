The Deadliest Streets of CuritibaArticle

Every fatal traffic crash in Curitiba has a coordinate. Stack fifteen years of them on the map and the city's traffic stops being anecdote and becomes geography: a single walkable downtown block emerges as the most lethal spot in town, the world-famous BRT busway shows up seven times in the fifteen worst segments, and the answer to "where is the most dangerous stretch" turns out to be three different questions with three different fixes. Includes a 3D map where every crash is a human figure.

What this is

A spatial investigation of road deaths in Brazil's model transit city. Curitiba recorded 2,947 fatal traffic crashes between 2010 and 2024, killing 3,073 people; 2,843 of those crashes carry exact coordinates. This project groups them by proximity, so that everything within roughly 140 meters becomes one "segment", then counts, names each segment through reverse geocoding, and asks the question every driver in the city has an opinion about: which stretch of road kills the most?

The answer is not a highway curve. It is the block around Rua Barão do Serro Azul, in the dense, walkable historic center: 25 fatal crashes within a 140 meter radius, 19 of them pedestrians struck by vehicles. Below the top spot, two names repeat across the ranking. The exclusive BRT busway, the corridor system that made Curitiba a global planning reference, appears seven times among the fifteen worst segments. The high-speed ring road (Contorno Leste) appears three more. Density, crossing, and speed: three distinct ways the city kills, each demanding a different fix.

The centerpiece is a 3D crash-density map in the spirit of ISOTYPE pictograms: one human figure for each fatal crash, stacked over the exact cell where it happened, colored by crash type, filterable, and rendered with MapLibre GL and deck.gl over a light basemap. The numbers stop being numbers when they have shoulders and a head.

Inside the analysis

  • From dashboard to coordinates. The source is IPPUC's official "Acidente de Trânsito" panel (BPTRAN / SMDT data), extracted programmatically from the published Tableau visualization, cleaned, and validated: 2,843 of 2,947 fatal crashes (96.5%) carry usable coordinates.
  • Proximity clustering, then naming. Crashes are grouped within a ~140 m radius into 1,561 distinct sites; each of the top sites is named by reverse geocoding its centroid against OpenStreetMap, so the ranking reads as streets and neighborhoods, not lat/lon pairs.
  • The finding. The most lethal spot is not where intuition points. It is the city's busiest pedestrian area: the historic center, where three in four deaths are people on foot. The BRT busway kills at the crossings between stations; the ring road kills at speed. Same question, three different geometries of risk.
  • Three problems, three fixes. Where density kills (downtown), the levers are protected crossings, signal timing and lower speeds. Where crossing kills (busway), they are safe crossings tied to the tube stations. Where speed kills (ring road), they are enforcement and road engineering. A single "most dangerous stretch" headline would flatten exactly the distinction that matters for policy.
  • Making the numbers human. The 3D map bins all 2,843 crashes into ~165 m cells and stacks one pictogram figure per crash, colored by type (pedestrians struck, collisions, other), with per-cell tooltips, category filters, and a 2D/3D toggle.

Methodology

Points were extracted from the IPPUC public panel via its underlying VizQL API, deduplicated and filtered to fatal crashes with valid coordinates inside the municipality. Hotspot detection uses greedy proximity clustering with a ~140 m radius, deliberately simple and auditable: each cluster is a place a traffic engineer can stand in, not a statistical abstraction. Segment names come from reverse geocoding each cluster centroid with OSM Nominatim and indicate the dominant road, not necessarily the exact corner. The 3D density map uses a separate ~165 m grid binning so every crash is drawn exactly once, one figure per crash, stacked bottom-up with the dominant crash type at the base.

Honest limits, stated on every page: the analysis covers fatal crashes only (non-fatal crashes follow different spatial patterns), 3.5% of fatal crashes could not be georeferenced, and a crash may kill more than one person, which is why 2,947 crashes correspond to 3,073 deaths. All figures are reproducible from the JSON extracts with two small Python build scripts; the maps are static HTML files with no backend.
